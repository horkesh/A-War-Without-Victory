# HVO (Hrvatsko Vijeće Obrane) - Master Order of Battle
## Croatian Defense Council 1992-1995

**Source Authority:** Balkan Battlegrounds Vol I & II, CIA Analysis

---

## SUPREME COMMAND STRUCTURE

**Political Leadership:**
- **Croatian Republic of Herzeg-Bosnia (self-declared entity)**
- President: Mate Boban (1992-Feb 1994)
- President: Krešimir Zubak (Feb 1994-1995)

**Military Command:**
- Commander: General Milivoj Petković (1992-1995)
- Deputy: Various
- HQ: Mostar (west bank)

**Croatian Government Support:**
- President of Croatia: Franjo Tuđman
- Croatian Army (HV) liaison and support
- Defense Minister coordination

**Strategic Relationship:**
- Close ties to Croatian Army (HV)
- Supplied from Croatia
- Officers often from Croatian Army
- Operations coordinated with Zagreb

---

## STRATEGIC CONTEXT

**Formation:**
- Formed April 1992 as defense force for Bosnian Croats
- Self-declared Croatian Republic of Herzeg-Bosnia (1993)
- Claimed to defend Croat-majority areas
- Ambiguous: Ally or rival to ARBiH?

**Evolution:**
- **1992:** Cooperation with ARBiH against VRS (uneasy alliance)
- **April 1993-Feb 1994:** War with ARBiH (brutal conflict)
- **Feb 1994-1995:** Federation alliance with ARBiH (Washington Agreement)

**Territory:**
- Western Herzegovina (Croat majority)
- Central Bosnia enclaves (surrounded by ARBiH)
- Posavina pockets (isolated)
- ~10-12% of BiH territory at peak

---

## ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURE

HVO organized by **Operational Zones** rather than corps:

1. **Southeast Herzegovina OZ** (Mostar) - Largest, strongest
2. **Central Bosnia OZ** (Vitez/Busovača) - Enclaves, besieged 1993-1994
3. **Northwest Bosnia OZ** (Posavina) - Isolated pockets
4. **Tomislavgrad OZ** (Western Herzegovina)

**Total Strength Evolution:**
- April 1992: ~25,000-35,000
- 1992: ~40,000-45,000
- 1993: ~50,000-55,000 (peak, war with ARBiH)
- 1994-1995: ~50,000-55,000 (Federation component)

## SOUTHEAST HERZEGOVINA OPERATIONAL ZONE (MOSTAR)

**HQ:** Mostar (west bank)
**Area:** Western Herzegovina, Mostar west
**Mission:** Control Croatian-majority areas, corridor to Croatia

**Commanders:**
- Various (formal structure developed 1993)

**Strength Evolution:**
- 1992: ~12,000-15,000
- 1993-1994: ~18,000-20,000 (war with ARBiH)
- 1995: ~20,000-22,000 (Federation)

**Assessment:** Strongest HVO operational zone, best equipped, supplied from Croatia

### Key Operations:
- **Mostar Division (1993):** City split along Neretva River
- **Siege of East Mostar (May 1993-Feb 1994):** HVO besieged ARBiH-held east bank
- **Stari Most Destruction (Nov 9, 1993):** HVO artillery destroyed iconic bridge
- **Post-Washington Agreement (1994-1995):** Integration with Federation

### Major Brigades:

**Mostar Brigade:**
- Strength: ~2,000-3,000
- Base: Mostar west bank
- Mission: City defense, siege operations

**Široki Brijeg Brigade:**
- Base: Široki Brijeg (HVO stronghold)
- Strength: ~1,500-2,500

**Ljubuški Brigade:**
- Base: Ljubuški
- Strength: ~1,500-2,000

**Grude Brigade:**
- Base: Grude area

**Čitluk Brigade:**
- Base: Čitluk area

**Čapljina Brigade:**
- Base: Čapljina

**Stolac Units:**
- Area: Stolac (contested with VRS)

**Area Control:**

**Firmly Controlled:**
- **Mostar (west bank and southern suburbs):** HVO stronghold
  - West bank: Croat majority
  - Controlled water, electricity for entire city
  - Used as leverage in siege
- **Široki Brijeg (municipality):** Main HVO base in Herzegovina
- **Grude (municipality):** Croat stronghold
- **Ljubuški (municipality):** Major garrison
- **Čitluk (municipality):** Croat area
- **Čapljina (municipality and town):** Strategic position, railway
- **Neum:** Corridor to Adriatic Sea

**Full brigade-level list (historical):** Kralj Petar Krešimir IV Brigade; Kralj Tomislav Brigade (Tomislavgrad)—Posušje battalion, Kupres battalion; Ban Jelačić Brigade; Herceg Stjepan Brigade; Mario Hrkač Čikota Brigade; 6th Brigade "Ranko Boban"; 1st Herzegovina Brigade "Knez Domagoj" (Čapljina); 1st Brigade (Mostar); 2nd Brigade (Mostar); 94th Brigade (Kiseljak); 95th Brigade (Kreševo–Fojnica); 101st–106th Brigades; 108th Brigade; 111th Brigade (Žepče). *Source: Balkan Battlegrounds narrative; supplementary: vojska.net HVO brigade list. BB Vol. I appendices do not include a dedicated HVO skeleton OOB.*

**Contested:**
- **Stolac:** Contested with VRS
- **Mostar east bank:** ARBiH held (besieged by HVO 1993-1994)

**Siege of East Mostar (May 1993-Feb 1994):**
- HVO completely surrounded east bank
- ~55,000 civilians trapped
- Starvation, daily shelling
- Humanitarian catastrophe
- Stari Most destroyed Nov 9, 1993 (major war crime)
- International outcry
- Siege ended with Washington Agreement Feb 1994

**Equipment:**
- Infantry weapons
- Artillery: 105mm, 122mm (Croatian supply)
- Mortars: 82mm, 120mm
- Tanks: ~20-30 (T-55)
- APCs: ~50-80 vehicles
- Better supplied than ARBiH via Croatia

**Strategic Importance:**
- Corridor to Croatia (supply route)
- Croat-majority region
- Economic importance (Čapljina, Mostar)
- Political capital of Herzeg-Bosnia

**Croatian Army (HV) Support:**
- Artillery support from Croatia
- Logistics, ammunition
- Officers, advisors
- Coordinated operations

## CENTRAL BOSNIA OPERATIONAL ZONE (VITEZ-BUSOVAČA)

**HQ:** Vitez, later Busovača
**Commander:** Colonel Tihomir Blašković (later Brigadier General)

**Area:** Central Bosnia enclaves (surrounded by ARBiH 3rd Corps)
**Mission:** Defense of Croat-majority enclaves

**Strength Evolution:**
- 1992: ~8,000-12,000
- 1993: ~10,000-12,000 (besieged, fighting ARBiH)
- 1994-1995: ~10,000-12,000 (Federation, enclaves remain)

**Assessment:** Surrounded enclaves, besieged 1993-1994, helicopter supplied

### Key Operations:
- **Ahmići Massacre (April 16, 1993):** HVO killed ~100+ Bosniak civilians (major war crime)
- **Defense of Enclaves (1993-1994):** Against ARBiH 3rd/7th Corps
- **Helicopter Supply (1993-1994):** Croatian helicopters from Split
- **Post-Washington Agreement (1994-1995):** Integration with Federation

### Major Brigades:

**"Vitezovi" Brigade (Vitez):**
- Strength: ~1,500-2,000
- Elite unit, best-equipped Central Bosnia HVO
- Base: Vitez town
- Controversial: Ahmići Massacre participants

**"Jure Francetić" Brigade (Busovača):**
- Strength: ~1,500-2,000
- Base: Busovača
- Named after Ustaša commander (controversial)

**"Stjepan Tomašević" Brigade (Novi Travnik):**
- Strength: ~1,000-1,500
- Base: Novi Travnik
- Heavy fighting with ARBiH 1993

**Kiseljak Brigade:**
- Strength: ~2,000-3,000
- Base: Kiseljak (isolated pocket south of Sarajevo)
- Separated from main Central Bosnia enclave

**Kreševo Brigade:**
- Base: Kreševo area
- Strength: ~1,000-1,500

**Travnik Brigade:**
- Expelled from Travnik by ARBiH 1993
- Remnants in Novi Travnik area

**Area Control:**

**Vitez-Busovača-Novi Travnik Enclave:**
- **Vitez (municipality and town):** HVO stronghold
  - Population: ~15,000-20,000 Croats
  - Surrounded by ARBiH 3rd Corps
  - Under siege 1993-1994
- **Busovača (municipality and town):** HVO stronghold
  - Population: ~10,000-15,000 Croats
  - Besieged 1993-1994
- **Novi Travnik (town):** HVO controlled
  - Mixed Croat-Bosniak town, heavy fighting 1993
  - Population: ~8,000-10,000 Croats

**Total Enclave:**
- Size: ~150-200 km²
- Population: ~30,000-40,000 Croats
- Completely surrounded by ARBiH

**Kiseljak Enclave (Separate):**
- **Kiseljak (municipality):** HVO isolated pocket
  - South of Sarajevo, surrounded by ARBiH 1st Corps
  - Size: ~100 km²
  - Population: ~8,000-12,000 Croats

**Lost Territory:**
- **Travnik:** ARBiH captured 1993 (expelled HVO)
- **Fojnica:** ARBiH captured 1993
- **Bugojno:** ARBiH captured July 1993 (heavy fighting)

**Siege Situation (1993-1994):**
- Surrounded by ARBiH 3rd/7th Corps
- No land supply route
- Helicopter supply from Croatia:
  - Flights from Split, Croatia
  - Dangerous, irregular
  - Limited capacity
- Civilians and military trapped
- Ceasefire Feb 1994 (Washington Agreement)

**Ahmići Massacre (April 16, 1993):**
- HVO "Vitezovi" Brigade attacked Ahmići village
- ~100-120 Bosniak civilians killed
- Houses burned, widespread destruction
- Major war crime
- Tihomir Blašković convicted at ICTY
- Poisoned HVO-ARBiH relations

**Equipment:**
- Infantry weapons (Croatian supply)
- Mortars: 82mm, 120mm
- Artillery: Limited, 105mm
- Tanks: ~5-10 (limited use in enclaves)
- Ammunition: Helicopter supplied (limited)

**Post-Washington Agreement (Feb 1994):**
- Ceasefire with ARBiH
- Enclaves remain, but siege ended
- Integration into Federation
- Deep mistrust persists
- HVO structure intact

**Strategic Situation:**
- Geographically isolated from main HVO (Herzegovina)
- Dependent on helicopter supply 1993-1994
- Political importance: Croat population enclaves
- Federation integration difficult (recent enemies)

## NORTHWEST BOSNIA OPERATIONAL ZONE (POSAVINA)

**Area:** Posavina region (Bosanski Brod, Odžak, Orašje)
**Mission:** Defense of pockets, cooperation with ARBiH against VRS

**Strength Evolution:**
- 1992: ~5,000-8,000
- 1993-1995: ~3,000-5,000 (most territory lost to VRS)

**Assessment:** Isolated pockets, heavy fighting 1992, mostly lost to VRS

### Key Operations:
- **Posavina Corridor Defense (April-July 1992):** Failed to hold against VRS/JNA
- **Orašje Pocket Defense (1992-1995):** Maintained small bridgehead
- **Cooperation with ARBiH:** Best cooperation in this sector

### Major Units:

**101st Orašje Brigade:**
- Strength: ~1,500-2,000
- Base: Orašje (bridgehead on Sava River)
- Mission: Hold pocket against VRS

**102nd Brigade:**
- Posavina area

**Bosanski Brod Units:**
- Remnants after VRS captured most of Bosanski Brod

**Area Control:**

**1992 (April-July):**
- Bosanski Brod, Odžak, Orašje, Derventa (contested)
- Joint HVO-ARBiH-HV defense against VRS 1st Krajina/East Bosnian Corps
- VRS "Corridor 92" offensive (June-July)
- Most territory lost to VRS

**1993-1995:**
- **Orašje Pocket:** Small HVO bridgehead
  - Size: ~50-100 km²
  - Population: ~10,000-15,000
  - On Sava River, Croatian border
  - Croatian Army (HV) artillery support across river
  - Supplied from Croatia
- **Bosanski Brod:** Small pocket (mostly lost)

**HVO Brigades Integrated with ARBiH:**

**107th "Gradačac" Brigade:**
- Strength: ~1,000-1,500
- Base: Gradačac (ARBiH 2nd Corps territory)
- **Status:** Operated under ARBiH 2nd Corps command throughout war
- Smooth cooperation, unique

**108th "Brčko" Brigade:**
- Strength: ~1,000-1,500
- Base: Brčko area (ARBiH 2nd Corps)
- **Status:** Operated under ARBiH 2nd Corps command throughout war
- Smooth cooperation

**Equipment:**
- Infantry weapons
- Mortars
- Limited heavy weapons
- Croatian support across Sava River (artillery)

**Strategic Situation:**
- Isolated pockets
- VRS controlled corridor (Brčko-Derventa)
- Orašje pocket maintained throughout war
- Best HVO-ARBiH cooperation (necessity)

## HVO BRIGADES IN ARBIH COMMAND STRUCTURE

**Unique Cooperation (Northeastern Bosnia):**

**115th "Zrinski" Brigade (Tuzla):**
- Strength: ~800-1,200
- Base: Tuzla (ARBiH 2nd Corps territory)
- Composition: Bosnian Croats from Tuzla
- **Status:** Operated under ARBiH 2nd Corps command (5th OG)
- Cooperation: Smooth 1992-1993
- **Disbanded:** January 1994 (Croat leadership ordered disbandment)
  - Most soldiers objected (wanted to continue with ARBiH)
  - Political decision by Herzeg-Bosnia leadership
  - Timing: Before Washington Agreement

**110th Usora Brigade:**
- Operated with ARBiH 2nd Corps
- Strength: ~500-1,000
- Cooperation throughout war

**Assessment:**
- These brigades showed cooperation was possible
- Northeastern Bosnia exception: Less ethnic tension
- Model for later Federation (1994-1995)

## HVO EQUIPMENT SUMMARY

**Small Arms:**
- Zastava M70, HK G3 (from Croatia)
- Standard infantry weapons
- Croatian supply

**Heavy Weapons:**
- **Artillery:** 105mm, 122mm howitzers (Croatian supply)
- **Mortars:** 82mm, 120mm (well-supplied)
- **Tanks:** ~50-80 total (T-55, some M-84)
- **APCs:** ~100-150 (M-80, BOV variants)

**Better Equipped than ARBiH:**
- Croatian supply lines functional
- No arms embargo (via Croatia)
- Better logistics

**Air Support:**
- Croatian helicopters (supply, some combat)
- No fixed-wing aircraft

**Anti-Tank:**
- RPG-7, AT weapons from Croatia

## HVO LOGISTICS AND SUPPORT

**Supply Routes:**
- **Primary:** Croatia → Western Herzegovina → Central Bosnia (via helicopter)
- **Secondary:** Croatian Army direct support

**Croatian Army (HV) Support:**
- **Critical relationship:** HVO effectively Croatian Army proxy
- Artillery support
- Logistics, ammunition, fuel
- Officer cadre, advisors
- Training in Croatia
- **Direct combat:** Croatian Army units fought in BiH
  - Bosanski Brod defense
  - 1995 offensives
  - Estimated ~20,000-40,000 HV troops in BiH operations at times

**Funding:**
- Croatian government
- Diaspora support
- Local taxation in Herzeg-Bosnia

## HVO STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

**Strengths:**
- Croatian government support (critical advantage)
- Better equipment than ARBiH (via Croatia)
- Professional officers (many from Croatian Army)
- Secure base (Western Herzegovina)
- No arms embargo (via Croatia)

**Weaknesses:**
- Small Croat population in BiH (~17% of total)
- Geographically divided (Herzegovina vs Central Bosnia enclaves)
- Dependence on Croatia
- Three-way war (vs VRS and ARBiH 1993-1994)
- International condemnation (Mostar siege, war crimes)

**Strategic Evolution:**

**1992: Ambiguous Ally**
- Cooperation with ARBiH against VRS (northeastern Bosnia, some areas)
- Cooperation with VRS against ARBiH (Kupres, some operations)
- Establishing Herzeg-Bosnia entity

**1993-Feb 1994: War with ARBiH**
- Brutal three-way war
- Central Bosnia enclaves besieged
- East Mostar siege
- Ahmići, other massacres
- Fighting on two fronts (VRS and ARBiH)

**Feb 1994-1995: Federation Alliance**
- Washington Agreement transformed situation
- Alliance with ARBiH (former enemy)
- Croatian Army direct support
- 1995 coordinated offensives
- Integration slow, mistrust deep

## HVO WAR CRIMES

**Major Incidents:**
- **Ahmići Massacre (April 16, 1993):** ~100-120 Bosniak civilians killed by HVO
- **Stari Most Destruction (Nov 9, 1993):** HVO artillery destroyed UNESCO heritage site
- **Stupni Do:** ARBiH response massacre (Oct 23, 1993) - ~30 Croat civilians
- **East Mostar Siege:** Humanitarian catastrophe, ~2,000 civilian deaths
- **Ethnic Cleansing:** Expulsion of Bosniaks from Croat-majority areas

**ICTY Convictions:**
- Tihomir Blašković (Central Bosnia commander) - convicted
- Dario Kordić (political leader) - convicted
- Mladen Naletilić "Tuta" (paramilitary) - convicted
- Slobodan Praljak (HVO general) - convicted, suicide in court
- Others

## CROATIAN REPUBLIC OF HERZEG-BOSNIA

**Political Entity:**
- Self-declared 1993
- President: Mate Boban (until Feb 1994)
- Capital: Mostar (west)
- Currency: Croatian Kuna
- Institutions: Parallel government structures

**Territory Claimed:**
- All Croat-majority areas in BiH
- ~30% of BiH claimed
- Actually controlled ~10-12%

**Status:**
- Not internationally recognized
- Dissolved with Washington Agreement (Feb 1994)
- Merged into Federation of BiH
- HVO became Federation military component

## FEDERATION PERIOD (FEB 1994-1995)

**Washington Agreement Impact:**
- HVO became part of Federation armed forces
- Political: Croatian Republic of Herzeg-Bosnia dissolved
- Military: Separate HVO command initially, gradual integration
- Operations: Coordinated with ARBiH, Croatian Army

**Integration Challenges:**
- Deep mistrust (recent enemies)
- War crimes (Ahmići, Mostar) fresh memories
- Territorial disputes
- Command structure unclear
- Weapons handover issues

**1995 Operations:**
- Croatian Army + HVO + ARBiH coordinated offensives
- Western Bosnia offensives (Aug-Sept)
- Pressure on Banja Luka
- Successful territorial gains

---

## HISTORICAL NOTES

1. HVO fought three-way war: Sometimes with ARBiH vs VRS, sometimes with VRS vs ARBiH, sometimes both
2. Washington Agreement (Feb 1994) transformed HVO from ARBiH enemy to ally
3. Croatian Army support was critical to HVO capability
4. Central Bosnia enclaves survived siege 1993-1994 via helicopter supply
5. HVO-ARBiH war (1993-1994) weakened both against VRS
6. Many HVO commanders convicted of war crimes at ICTY
7. Federation integration slow, trust-building difficult
8. HVO effectively Croatian Army proxy force in BiH
